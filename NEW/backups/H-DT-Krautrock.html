Krautrock was a music subgenre that emerged from West Germany in the late 1960s and early 1970s, blending psychedelic rock with avant-garde influences. Rejecting the Anglo-American rock tradition, its artists embraced long-form jams, hypnotic rhythms, electronic textures, and repetition. Key inspirations included Pink Floyd, The Velvet Underground, composer Karlheinz Stockhausen, minimalists like Terry Riley, and even James Brown's funk rhythms. The radical student protest movements of late 1960s West Germany also shaped the scene culturally. The term "Krautrock" was coined by the British press and considered derogatory by some, leading many artists to prefer the alternative name Kosmische Musik ("cosmic music").

Two major regional scenes emerged from Krautrock. The Düsseldorf School, rooted in the experimental teachings of Joseph Beuys at the Düsseldorf Art Academy, was defined by its iconic motorik beat — a hypnotic, circular 4/4 drum pattern pioneered by Klaus Dinger of NEU! The scene's most influential act, Kraftwerk, evolved from experimental rock roots into a fully synthesizer-based sound by the mid-1970s, becoming a foundational influence on synthpop and techno. Other key Düsseldorf figures included NEU!, Cluster, the supergroup Harmonia, and producer Conny Plank, who helped shape and connect the scene. Its later phase saw the rise of industrial acts like DAF and Die Krupps, though these largely rejected their predecessors.

The Berlin School, by contrast, took Krautrock in a more atmospheric and spacious direction. Led by artists such as Tangerine Dream, Klaus Schulze, and Manuel Göttsching, it prioritized texture and ambience over rhythm, making it a precursor to ambient, electronica, new age, and trance music.

Together, these two scenes demonstrate the remarkable breadth of Krautrock's legacy, with the Düsseldorf School shaping the rhythmic and electronic backbone of modern pop and dance music, and the Berlin School laying the groundwork for more introspective and atmospheric electronic genres.
